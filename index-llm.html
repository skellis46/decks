AI & LLM Use in PGR Research

C-DaRE · Postgraduate Researcher Survey · Spring 2026

Findings, tensions, and what they mean for institutional guidance

Frame: this is the first systematic look at how our doctoral researchers actually relate to these tools. ~1 min intro.

The study

What we asked, and who answered

  • Anonymous questionnaire to all 45 C-DaRE postgraduate researchers
  • 26 responded — a 58% response rate
  • 8 sections: usage, tools, attitudes, ethics, authorship, quality, support needs, open text

Small-n, so treat figures as indicative rather than definitive — but the patterns are consistent and strongly held.

Stress the qualitative weight: with this cohort the open responses carry as much signal as the numbers.

The cohort in one line

Low-confidence, ethically anxious, largely unsupported

  • 2.0/5 — mean confidence
  • 3.9/5 — mean ethical concern
  • 12% — report clear institutional guidance
This is the headline. Everything that follows unpacks these three numbers.

Engagement (Q1)

Reluctant, not enthusiastic

  • 1 uses LLMs regularly
  • 13 occasionally
  • 9 tried them and stopped
  • 3 never have

Half the cohort has either withdrawn or never engaged. This is not a story of rapid adoption.

The "tried and stopped" group is analytically important — withdrawal is an active stance here, not inertia.

Tools & uses (Q2–Q3)

Concentrated, and ChatGPT-dominated

  • ChatGPT leads (18); Claude second (6); Copilot just 3
  • Main uses: literature search/summarising (10), writing/editing (8), explaining concepts (7)
  • Used early (scoping) and late (editing) — rarely in the analytical middle

Note the tool mismatch: the institutionally provisioned tool (Copilot) is not the one students reach for.

The middle-stage avoidance matters: students keep AI away from the core intellectual work. Foreshadows "writing as thinking."

Attitudes (Q5 & Q7)

Low confidence sits beside high concern

  • 14 of 26 rated confidence at the floor (1/5); no one chose 5
  • 11 of 26 rated ethical concern at the ceiling (5/5)
  • Top worries: academic integrity (65% very concerned), hallucinations (62%)

Concern about unclear guidelines ranks lower — so ethical worry isn't merely a symptom of missing rules.

Set up the key point: this anxiety is principled, not just procedural confusion.

The central paradox

Concern is highest among non-users

  • Non-users / stopped: ethical concern 4.0–4.3; occasional users 3.5
  • Confidence and ethical concern are inversely correlated (r = −0.49)
The more capable students feel, the less they worry.
This is the analytically sharpest finding. Pause here.

Why it matters

Competence training is not ethics training

  • Ethical worry appears to drive avoidance — it's a reason for non-use, not abstract attitude
  • The prescription "build skills and confidence" may lower ethical vigilance if delivered without ethics framing
  • The two cannot be treated as separate tracks
Direct policy implication. The standard "just train them" response is actively undermined by this data.

The ethical line (Q8)

Consensus on one thing, uncertainty beyond

  • 92% flag submitting LLM-generated text as one's own
  • But the cohort splits on: brainstorming (42%), structuring arguments (42%)
  • Only 15% see grammar-checking as problematic

Genuine, unresolved disagreement about where the line falls — not ignorance of it.

The clean consensus masks deep divergence the moment you move past the obvious case.

The ownership paradox (Q10)

Strongest convictions, least experience

  • "Never" users anticipate the greatest loss of ownership: 4.67/5
  • Occasional users feel least affected: 2.31/5

Intuition runs ahead of experience — a principled, pre-reflective stance on authorship, not a verdict learned from use.

Reinforces that refusal here is conviction-driven.

Voices (Q12 & Q15)

Writing is the thinking

The most pervasive theme — ~10 responses, across all usage groups.

It simply badly replaces the thinking work that I need to do myself, which in fact constitutes the research work.

Outsourcing the writing isn't a shortcut — it bypasses the intellectual process that constitutes the research.

This directly contests any guidance that frames LLM rephrasing as harmless polishing.

Voices

The boundary blur problem

The line of when are we using AI is a bit blurred.
  • Grammarly, AI search, embedded features feel categorically different from ChatGPT — yet are all "AI"
  • Disclosure is meaningless without a workable definition of "LLM use"

A practical definition is a prerequisite for any policy — not a footnote to it.

Without resolving this, no disclosure framework can be applied consistently or assessed fairly.

Voices

Refusal has many roots

  • Environmental & ethical burden — principled withdrawal, not inconvenience
  • Sensitive participant data — a near-universal red line
  • Voice & authenticity — fear of homogenised, "edited-the-edit" output
  • FOMO vs. stigma — pressure to adopt and suspicion of anyone who does

Avoidance looks the same on the surface; the reasons require different institutional responses.

Don't collapse these into "resistance." Pragmatic and principled objections need different answers.

The gap (Q13)

Navigating high stakes without support

  • 12% report clear guidance (3 of 26)
  • 62% are unaware of guidance or find it vague

Students are making consequential decisions on ethics, disclosure and integrity — with little to guide them.

The starkest finding. Tie back to the anxiety: high concern + no support.

What students want (Q14)

Examples, not edicts

  • Discipline-specific examples of good practice — 46% (top request)
  • Workshops (27%) · Peer discussion / community of practice (27%)
  • Clearer policy ranks lower

They want contextualised guidance for arts and performance research — not generic policy statements.

Embodied knowledge, practice-as-research, non-Western traditions, human participants — none generic.

Recommendations

Five priorities

  1. Develop discipline-specific guidance — generic policy won't fit this work
  2. Define "LLM use" practically — a prerequisite for disclosure
  3. Pair competence with ethics — never train one without the other
  4. Create space for peer dialogue — surface practice where stigma silences it
  5. Acknowledge the full range of stances — refusers, adopters, the ambivalent
These follow directly from the tensions, not from external opinion.

The takeaway

There is no single "LLM user" here

Reluctant adopters, principled refusers, pragmatic users, and those who tried and stopped — for very different reasons. Guidance that assumes enthusiasm, or assumes resistance, will fail.

Close on the diversity of positions as the starting point for any policy. Leave time for questions.
