

# C-DaRE  
## Field-leading dance research, embodied methods, wider impact

Note:
This is a vision for positioning C-DaRE over the next five years. The emphasis is on how the Centre can build from its existing success, make its value clearer, and develop the next phase of its research leadership.

---

## Central proposition

C-DaRE should lead dance research by showing that dance is not only an object of study, but a way of producing methods, evidence and insight about embodied human experience.

Note:

The task is not to move from dance research to something else.

It is to position dance research as a field with distinctive methods, knowledge and applications that are increasingly needed by other fields.

I want to hold two things together. 

First, C-DaRE must remain visibly and confidently rooted in dance research: for example somatic practices, inclusive practices, practice research, dance heritage, dance and technology. Second, it must show more clearly how dance research contributes to wider challenge-led contexts. Dance first; wider consequence second.

---

## Why this matters now

- health, care and wellbeing
- AI, robotics and digital technologies
- creative cultures and cultural resilience
- disability, access and inclusion
- heritage, memory and cultural identity

Note:
Major contemporary questions are also questions of embodied human experience.

C-DaRE has the expertise to address these questions without diluting dance research.

The external research environment increasingly asks for challenge-led, transdisciplinary and impact-oriented work. Dance research can look marginal if it is described only as a specialist cultural field. But C-DaRE’s work shows that dance research has methods and knowledge that matter to urgent wider questions. The point is not to chase fashion. The point is to explain better what we already know how to do.

---

## The productive tension

Some of the most important dance research is not easily translated into instrumental funding language.

A healthy research centre must therefore do two things:

- protect long-term artistic, critical, archival, somatic and practice-based enquiry;
- build credible routes to partnerships, income, impact and public value.

This tension is not a problem to remove. It is a condition to lead well.

Note:
This is an important leadership issue. Not everything valuable in dance research starts with a funder category. Some of the field’s deepest work takes time and moves through artistic, cultural, embodied and critical enquiry. But that does not absolve us from the need to build income, partnerships and impact. The role of the Centre Director is to hold this tension honestly and productively.

---

## C-DaRE already demonstrates this

Examples across the Centre show dance research operating at full strength in wider contexts:

- AI, robotics, disability and access;
- Roma communities, identity and dance;
- somatic practice and chronic pain;
- dance heritage, cultural memory and DanceMap;
- participatory practice in creative cultures;
- practice research as rigorous knowledge production.

These are not departures from dance research. They show what dance research can know, do and change.

Note:
This is where I would briefly name colleagues and projects, without turning the slide into a list of everyone in the Centre. The point is that we already have strong evidence. Whatley and Marsh’s work, Cisneros’ work, Meehan’s work, Crawley and DanceMap, Stamp’s work, Burrows and practice research all show that C-DaRE is already doing this. The next step is to position it more clearly.

---

## Principle 1: consolidate field leadership

Build from C-DaRE’s existing strength.

Priorities:

- maintain national and international leadership in dance research;
- support excellence-led REF outputs and practice research quality;
- protect the Centre’s collegial, ambitious and intellectually serious culture;
- build on PGR and ECR momentum as field-shaping capacity;
- sustain visible leadership in journals, conferences, networks, examining and advisory roles.

Note:
The first principle is consolidation. C-DaRE is already strong. I would not present the next five years as a rescue mission or an identity change. We need to maintain the conditions that already make the Centre successful: research quality, collegiality, doctoral culture, practice research expertise, and international credibility.

---

## Principle 2: extend reach through embodied methods

C-DaRE’s wider contribution is methodological as well as thematic.

It offers rigorous ways of researching:

- movement, sensation and attention;
- embodiment, care and vulnerability;
- participation, relation and identity;
- artistic practice as research evidence;
- the human dimensions of technological and social change.

This creates stronger routes into health, AI/digital technologies, creative cultures, heritage and wellbeing.

Note:
This slide is crucial. I would stress that C-DaRE does not only contribute topics. It contributes methods. Other fields often recognise that bodies, movement, experience and relation matter, but they do not always have the tools to study them. Dance research does. That is one of C-DaRE’s strongest routes into interdisciplinary work.

---

## Principle 3: make value legible

C-DaRE’s work is strong, but its wider value is not always immediately understood outside dance research.

Leadership task: articulation.

Priorities:

- complete the Centre communications and positioning review;
- develop one clear proposition for what dance research does;
- produce concrete examples for funders, university leadership, other disciplines, publics and partners;
- increase visibility in selected professional, policy-facing and interdisciplinary contexts.

Note:
This connects directly to work already underway in the Centre. The question is not just “what do we do?” but “how do we make that intelligible to different audiences without flattening it?” The Centre needs a core proposition, a small set of strong examples, and audience-specific language. That will support bids, partnerships, internal visibility and external recognition.

---

## Five-year trajectory

### Year 1: articulate and align

Map current research, bids, partnerships, PGR activity and visibility against the three principles.

### Year 3: consolidate and test

Develop durable partnerships in selected areas: embodied technologies, health/wellbeing, heritage and cultural resilience.

### Year 5: establish the next phase

C-DaRE is recognised as a centre that leads dance research and demonstrates its wider methodological and applied significance.

Note:
The timeline should be light and credible. Year 1 is about mapping, articulation, baseline data and agreeing priorities. Year 3 is about testing what has worked, strengthening selected partnerships, and learning from the current REF cycle. Year 5 is about a clearer position nationally and internationally. I would avoid over-complicated targets here.

---

## What success would look like

A mixed evidence base:

- sustained excellence in dance research and practice research;
- stronger and more diverse bid and partnership pipeline;
- increased invitations from dance and non-dance contexts;
- clear Centre proposition and case studies in use;
- healthy PGR and ECR pipeline;
- stronger recognition within CU Group priorities;
- better evidence for impact, environment and future REF cycles.

Note:
This answers the “how will we know?” question without reducing the vision to a spreadsheet. Some indicators are quantitative: bid numbers, income, PGR completions, partnerships, invitations. Others are qualitative: better recognition, stronger examples, clearer narratives, improved practice research evidencing. Both matter.

---

## Leadership approach

Culture is not separate from strategy.

It is one of the conditions through which quality, income, impact and sustainability become possible.

My approach would be to combine:

- clarity about direction and expectations;
- support for colleagues’ distinctive strengths;
- collective ownership of bids, outputs and impact;
- honest attention to workload, wellbeing and sustainability;
- stronger articulation of C-DaRE’s value inside and beyond the Group.

Note:
This slide should connect vision to leadership. The Centre Director role is not only about strategy documents. It is about getting the best out of people, supporting ambition, making decisions, holding accountability, and keeping the Centre’s culture healthy. Bid-writing retreats, PGR leadership, practice research leadership and the communications work all provide evidence for this approach.

---

## Closing vision

My vision is not to redirect C-DaRE away from dance research.

It is to position C-DaRE as a Centre that leads the field precisely because it can show why dance research matters:

as artistic and cultural enquiry,  
as practice research,  
as embodied method,  
and as a way of addressing urgent questions in health, technology, disability, heritage, identity and cultural life.

Note:
End by returning to the central argument. Dance remains the foundation. The next five years are about clearer positioning, stronger articulation, strategic partnerships, a more diverse pipeline, and confident leadership of dance research as a field whose methods and insights are needed beyond itself.
